# Respite — Vision

> *"Make the night count."* — sleep, rest, and the passage of night, made worth living through.

## The promise

Respite takes over the night. Vanilla's bed is a skip button — you click, the screen fades, and morning is simply there, as if the dark hours never happened. Respite makes them happen: sleeping rushes time visibly past instead of jumping over it, a hearty supper heals you while you dream, too many days without rest wear you down, and the clock itself becomes something you can build with and brew against. Sleep stops being a way to avoid the game and becomes part of playing it. If you have ever wished the night were real time instead of a fade to black, this is for you.

## The arc of a world

Your first night, you climb into bed and the world does not cut away. Stars streak overhead, your furnace roars through its queue, rain sweeps in and out — and dawn arrives in seconds, watched rather than skipped. Within a few days you have the new habits: eat a full meal before bed and wake with your hearts back, load the furnaces before you lie down, glance at the moon before planning a mountain trip.

By mid game you are building with time itself. A Chronometer beside your door bolts it at dusk and lights the lanterns; another meters the farm. A cocoa grove keeps you in Caffeinated Brew for the nights a project cannot wait, and you have learned which nights those can safely be — never a new moon, when phantoms own the whole sky, though a battered crew learns to love that night from indoors, where sleep runs deepest.

Mastery is a rhythm. On a shared server, night becomes a negotiation — every player in bed speeds the dark along, so "who's sleeping?" is a real question with a real payoff. You know the weariness rules well enough to push three days at a bench and no further, and your high-mountain outpost is walled against the phantoms that patrol its altitude. The night is no longer a pause in your world. It is a working part of it.

## The features, as you'll play them

### Continuous time-lapse

- **What it is** — Sleeping no longer teleports the world to morning. It accelerates time — up to 60× — while the world keeps genuinely running: furnaces smelt, crops grow, brewing stands bubble, and the sky wheels overhead.
- **How you use it** — Get into a bed at night, as ever. Each sleeper adds an equal share of the speed: alone in your world you get the full 60×, and on a server with two of four players in bed, time runs at half that. Leave the bed and time eases back to normal.
- **What changes for you** — A full night passes in about ten seconds with everyone asleep, and the world lives through all of it — a furnace smelts close to a full stack while you dream. Sleep becomes the most productive thing you can do after dark, and players still awake share the fast night on their own terms: your hunger and your potion timers keep to real time while the world races, and the moment a monster actually comes for someone, time holds at normal pace until the fight is settled. A crew decides together when to let the dark rush by.

### Restful saturation

- **What it is** — Going to bed on a full stomach heals you overnight: the saturation from your last meals converts into health while you sleep. And on a new moon — the darkest night, one in eight — sleep runs deepest: the same reserve heals double.
- **How you use it** — Eat until your hunger bar is full — the heartier the meal, the deeper the reserve — then sleep through the night. Every 2 points of saturation restore 1 heart by morning; under a new moon, every 2 points restore 2.
- **What changes for you** — A supper of steak or golden carrots can carry you from a battle-worn half-health to full hearts by dawn, so cooking well before bed is mechanically worth it. The reserve is spent doing it: you wake healed, but hungrier than you lay down. And the new moon becomes a night with two plans on it: outside, phantoms own the whole sky; in bed, an ordinary supper heals like a feast, and you wake deeply rested.

### Phantoms of the heights

- **What it is** — Insomnia is gone: phantoms never spawn because you skipped sleep. They are instead a natural nocturnal hazard with a territory — the high sky above Y=100 on any night, and everywhere under a new moon, one night in eight.
- **How you use it** — Check your altitude and the moon before committing to a night outdoors. Below the peaks on an ordinary night you will never hear that shriek; on a summit, or anywhere when the moon is dark, come armored or come inside.
- **What changes for you** — Phantoms stop being a punishment for how you play and become a place and a time: mountains and sky bases have a native guardian, new-moon nights are events worth planning around, and phantom membranes are earned by climbing, not by staying up out of spite.

### Weariness

- **What it is** — Sleeplessness wears you down in two honest steps. Three in-game days without rest and you are Weary: your natural health regeneration slows by 25%. Push on to six and you are Exhausted: regeneration slows by 50%, and every couple of minutes your eyelids droop — the screen dims for half a second, never in a fight. No monsters are sent after you — nothing hunts the sleepless. The ladder runs the other way too: wake from a full night's sleep and you are Well-Rested for a couple of minutes, healing a little faster while the grace lasts.
- **How you use it** — You don't have to do anything; a status-effect icon appears like any other at each stage, the negative ones and the well-rested one alike. Any night's sleep — or a Caffeinated Brew — clears both weary stages and resets the count, and a full night in bed leaves the Well-Rested grace behind.
- **What changes for you** — Long expeditions and all-night builds carry a real, felt cost: Exhausted, the hearts you would shake off in fifteen seconds take thirty, so diving back into the cave at half health becomes a different decision. Rest is worth scheduling — but skipping it is a choice the game respects, not one it avenges: the blink waits for calm moments and never costs you a fight. And the morning after a real night's rest finally feels like something: you rise a touch quicker to heal, a small reward that never tips into making sleep a chore.

### The Chronometer

- **What it is** — A copper-and-redstone timepiece block whose face tracks the sun, emitting a redstone signal that climbs with the hour: strength 1 at dawn, 8 at dusk, 15 in the last stretch before sunrise, each level lasting 80 seconds.
- **How you use it** — Craft it from copper ingots, redstone, and a clock; place it and wire it like any redstone component. Pick the signal strengths that matter to you and build against them. A tap on the block tells you the hour — and after dark, the moon's phase and how many nights remain until the new moon.
- **What changes for you** — Time-of-day automation without daylight-sensor guesswork: doors that bolt at dusk, lanterns that light at nightfall, a farm that runs on a schedule, an alarm before the new-moon dark. The day becomes something your base can read.

### The Caffeinated Brew

- **What it is** — A bitter drink of cocoa and leaves that shakes off weariness: it clears the Weary effect, resets your days-awake count, and grants 90 seconds of Haste.
- **How you use it** — Steep two cocoa beans and leaves in a water bottle at a crafting table, then cook the bottle over a campfire for 30 seconds. Drink when the night's work cannot wait.
- **What changes for you** — All-nighters become safe and deliberate: a stocked cocoa grove lets you push through a third sleepless day at full healing speed, with a burst of Haste for the work itself. It postpones rest but never replaces it — only a real night's sleep converts a supper into hearts.

### The Bedroll

- **What it is** — A camp bed you carry: craft it from string and wool, and it unrolls onto any solid ground to sleep like a bed for the night, then rolls back into your pack when you wake.
- **How you use it** — On the road at nightfall, right-click the ground to unroll it and turn in — the same bed rules apply (night, no monsters at your back, under the open sky or a roof). It never sets your spawn, so morning still comes to you where you lie, not a new home. All the usual bed checks, none of the commitment.
- **What changes for you** — An expedition finally has a night. The time-lapse runs, your days-awake count resets, and a full stomach still heals you overnight — at half a real bed's rate, so a bed at home remains the better rest and a new moon under a bedroll heals like an ordinary night in your own bed. Camp light, sleep rough, and keep moving.

## Staying in control

Every headline feature — the time-lapse, overnight healing, the phantom rules, weariness, the Chronometer, the brew, the bedroll — can be switched off on its own, and the numbers a server plans around are tunable: the maximum acceleration (default 60×), whether a nearby battle holds the time-lapse (on by default), the weariness ladder (Weary at 3 days for a 25% healing slowdown, Exhausted at 6 days for 50%, and the Well-Rested morning grace — a 50% healing boost for two minutes after a night's sleep), the new-moon deep-sleep bonus (default double), the phantom altitude line (default Y=100), and how much of a bed's overnight healing a bedroll gives (default half). The Exhausted eyelid droop can be switched off on your own screen. A server that wants vanilla's insomnia back can turn it on. `/respite status` tells any player the current time-lapse speed, how long they have been awake, and how many nights until the new moon. Change nothing and you get all of Respite with insomnia retired — and everything outside the night untouched: world generation, mob behavior by day, and every block and item you already know work exactly as before.

## Better together

- **With Tribulation**, its difficulty scaling reaches Respite's phantoms — a new-moon summit late in a hardened world is a genuine fight, with drops to match. Never required.
- **With Mercantile**, the wandering trader and well-earned village standing can put cocoa and the makings of the brew on a counter near you. Never required.
- **With Prosperity**, a Chronometer or a bottle of the brew may turn up in a far-flung chest — the deep wilderness keeps its own time. Never required.
- **With Meridian**, nothing changes yet: enchanting is a craft of shelves and study, not of sleep, and Respite will not invent a coupling the game doesn't suggest.

With no siblings installed, nothing is missing — Respite's night is complete on its own.

## What Respite will never do

- **Never punish you with monsters.** Weariness slows your healing and nothing more; no creature is ever sent after the sleepless. How hard the world fights you is Tribulation's business, and Respite stays out of it.
- **Never touch what food is worth.** Hunger and saturation values stay vanilla's; Respite only gives a full stomach somewhere good to go. And the Caffeinated Brew is one drink, not a potion system — the brewing stand keeps its secrets.
- **Never reach into its siblings' rooms.** Villager trades and prices are Mercantile's, chest loot is Prosperity's, enchanting is Meridian's. Respite changes none of them.
- **No dream world.** Sleeping never takes you anywhere — no new dimensions, ever. Morning comes to you.
- **Never force the night on anyone.** No required companion mods, and no mandatory sleep: a player who never touches a bed loses only healing speed, never the game.
- **Nothing persistent on your screen.** No HUD element, no badge, no meter — weariness is an ordinary status-effect icon, the Chronometer's face and the sky itself are the only clocks, and the time-lapse is something you watch, not read off a meter. The one thing Respite ever paints over your view is your own heavy eyelids when you are Exhausted — half a second at a time, never in combat, and you can turn it off.
